Transparency, once introduced into ad exchanges, does not politely coexist with opacity. It destroys it.

That is the part many incumbents still seem unwilling to accept. They talk as if transparency is a feature toggle, something they can offer selectively, watermark heavily, rate-limit, surround with caveats, and maybe claw back later once buyers and sellers have “learned enough.” That is fantasy. In markets, transparency is not a product enhancement. It is a state change.

Once participants can see what was previously hidden, they reorganize around that knowledge. They renegotiate contracts. They change routing logic. They rewrite procurement rules. They stop accepting hand-wavy explanations for margins that used to be invisible. And most importantly, they begin to compare one venue against another on the dimensions that actually matter: price, fill, quality, latency, fraud, and take rate.

That process is irreversible because information changes expectations. Nobody who has seen the spread goes back to being comfortable with the story.

This is why I think ad exchange fees, wherever transparency becomes real, are headed in one direction: down. Not from 30% to 20%. Not from 30% to 15%. Down toward 2%, maybe with some specialized services priced separately, but with the core matching and routing function priced like infrastructure rather than mysticism.

If that sounds radical, it only sounds radical because digital advertising has spent a long time pretending not to be a market.

The best analogy is the shipping container.

Before standardized containers, moving goods was expensive, messy, and full of hidden frictions. Cargo had to be handled differently at every step. Ports, railroads, trucking firms, warehouses, and ships all had their own procedures. Labor was intensive. Delays were common. Theft was common. Damage was common. The interfaces between systems were where money disappeared.

The container did not merely make shipping more efficient. It standardized the unit of transfer. That forced the rest of the ecosystem to adapt. Ports had to change. Ships had to change. Cranes had to change. Railcars had to change. Warehouses had to change. But once the standard existed and enough of the network retooled around it, the economics of global trade changed permanently. You did not get to “unlearn” containerization and go back to artisanal cargo handling because some incumbent preferred the old margins.

Transparency in ad exchanges plays the same role. It standardizes visibility into what is being bought, what is being sold, who is taking a cut, and what performance actually looks like after all the intermediaries have had their turn. Once that visibility exists, market participants can finally treat ad inventory less like a magical black box and more like containerized freight: a thing that can be routed, compared, priced, and switched.

And when that happens, margins collapse.

High exchange fees survive in darkness. They require complexity, asymmetry, and switching pain. They require buyers to believe the exchange is doing something uniquely difficult and sellers to believe they have no practical alternative. They require just enough ambiguity that nobody can cleanly separate the commodity function from the premium story wrapped around it.

But matching buyers and sellers is not worth 30% in a transparent market. Not repeatedly. Not at internet scale. Not once the data makes clear how much of the fee is paying for genuine value versus historical leverage.

The defense of these fees is usually some variation of: “Yes, but the market is sticky.” True. Switching costs are real. They matter a lot. In fact, the entire debate turns on them.

Closed platforms love switching costs because switching costs are the closest thing to gravity in business. If a buyer has custom integrations, workflow dependencies, reporting pipelines, optimization models, compliance approvals, identity linkages, billing relationships, and operational muscle memory all tied to one exchange, then the platform can charge more than the raw service is worth. The customer is not just paying for execution. They are paying to avoid disruption.

This works until transparency changes the math.

Switching costs do not need to go to zero for fees to compress. They only need to become finite and legible. Once a customer can estimate, with reasonable confidence, “It will cost me X to move, and I will save Y every year by moving,” the platform loses its aura. It becomes a spreadsheet problem.

That is a terrible place to be if you’re charging 30%.

Suppose switching is painful. Fine. Maybe it costs a large advertiser millions in engineering time, retraining, temporary performance risk, and procurement overhead. That still does not save the incumbent if the recurring tax is large enough. A 30% take rate on substantial media volume is not a nuisance fee. It is a strategic liability. Once exposed, it invites unbundling, internal pressure, and procurement aggression. The customer may not move tomorrow, but they will absolutely begin building leverage. They will split spend. They will demand rebates. They will support standards. They will fund adapters. They will tolerate short-term migration pain in exchange for structural savings.

That is how fee compression begins. Not with idealized perfect competition, but with the first credible outside option.

Open protocols matter here more than people admit. They do not win because they are morally superior. They win because they make coordination cheaper.

A closed platform says: come into my system, use my tools, trust my measurement, accept my taxonomy, adapt to my roadmap, and if you ever want to leave, good luck carrying all that state with you.

An open protocol says: here is the interface. Anyone can implement it. Anyone can build on it. Anyone can compete at one layer without owning all layers. Your counterparties are not trapped inside a single company’s boundary.

That structure is brutally powerful over time because it shifts competition from access control to execution quality.

Closed platforms win early because they are simpler to launch and easier to orchestrate. They can move fast, subsidize one side of the market, and hide ugly details in the middle. They often feel better before the market matures because one company can paper over fragmentation. But once the market is large enough, the closed platform starts feeding on the very frictions it once reduced. Integration difficulty becomes a moat. Proprietary metrics become a moat. Data lock-in becomes a moat. Opaque pricing becomes a moat.

At that point, the platform is no longer just coordinating the market. It is taxing the market.

Open protocols beat closed platforms when the ecosystem becomes too important to be governed by a single toll collector. Email beat proprietary messaging networks. The web beat online services. TCP/IP beat vendor-specific networking stacks. Linux beat a thousand attempts to own the operating system layer of the internet. The pattern is not that open wins instantly. The pattern is that open wins when enough participants realize that interoperability compounds faster than control.

Ad exchanges are not exempt from that pattern. They are actually unusually vulnerable to it, because the core service they provide is closer to logistics than to art. Route demand. expose supply. clear transactions. enforce standards. report outcomes. Those are important functions, but important does not mean entitled to luxury margins forever.

The container analogy matters again here. Once containers existed, value did not disappear from shipping. It moved. Some value stayed in transport. Some moved to scale operators. Some moved to software and coordination. Some moved to financing, warehousing, and supply chain intelligence. But the basic act of moving a standardized unit through an interoperable network became massively cheaper.

That is what transparency will do in advertising.

It will not eliminate profit. It will eliminate unjustified profit hiding inside complexity.

Exchanges will still be able to charge for genuinely differentiated services. Better fraud prevention. Better identity resolution where legally permitted. Better forecasting. Better curation. Better guarantees. Better analytics. Better workflow software. Better credit and settlement. Better brand safety. Fine. Charge for those. But charge for them as products, not as camouflage for a bloated base tax on every transaction.

The base transaction itself should be cheap.

Why 2%? Not because 2% is a magical number, but because once a function becomes transparent, standardized, and contestable, economics start pulling it toward infrastructure pricing. If multiple providers can clear similar transactions using common protocols and customers can switch without rewriting their whole business, then price converges toward cost plus a modest margin. In digital markets, marginal costs are low, scale is high, and software is replicable. That does not support a permanent 30% rake unless the service is secretly doing something irreplaceable.

Usually it isn’t.

What it is doing is benefiting from old integration scars and old information asymmetries.

Those decay. Slowly at first, then all at once.

The incumbent response is predictable. They will say transparency is confusing. They will say customers misinterpret the data. They will say hidden complexity is necessary complexity. They will say the market values outcomes, not fee purity. They will say their bundled system performs better because it is integrated. Some of that will even be true. But none of it changes the directional outcome. Once the market can see enough to compare, pressure on the fee stack becomes relentless.

And importantly, transparency does not need to be perfect to be irreversible. It just needs to be good enough for participants to detect that spreads are wider than they thought and that alternatives are more viable than they were told.

That threshold is lower than incumbents hope.

The reason this matters goes beyond advertiser savings. Opaque ad markets don’t just enrich intermediaries; they distort product decisions across the ecosystem. Publishers optimize for whichever pipes are easiest to monetize, not necessarily the ones that create the most value for audiences. Buyers optimize around platform-specific quirks instead of underlying media quality. Measurement becomes political. Attribution becomes negotiable. Everyone overinvests in gaming the market because nobody fully trusts the market.

Transparency is the reset. It turns mythology back into economics.

And economics is unforgiving.

If one exchange can profitably operate at 5%, another will try 4%. If protocol-level interoperability reduces migration pain, someone will try 3%. If settlement, routing, and reporting become more standardized, somebody will make money at 2%. Maybe a few premium environments maintain more. Maybe certain high-touch managed services stay much higher. But the core exchange layer, the basic “we sit in the middle and clear trades” layer, will get pushed down toward commodity pricing because that is what transparent markets do to middlemen.

Not immediately. Irreversibly.

That is the key distinction. People hear “irreversible” and think “overnight.” No. Containers did not remake global shipping in a quarter. Open internet protocols did not erase proprietary networks in a year. But once the superior organizing principle arrives, the old model stops being the future. It becomes a legacy revenue stream waiting to be competed away.

That is where opaque, high-fee ad exchanges are headed.

The moment transparency becomes credible, the countdown starts. Buyers get more disciplined. Sellers get less captive. New entrants build against the protocol instead of begging for platform permission. Procurement gets involved. Finance gets involved. Engineers get involved. The fee stack, once hidden in jargon and workflow inertia, becomes visible enough to attack.

And once a market starts attacking a 30% take rate, it does not stop at 20% out of politeness.

It keeps going until the price resembles the actual function.

My bet is that for the exchange layer, that number looks a lot closer to 2% than 30%. Not because the industry suddenly becomes virtuous, but because transparency, like the shipping container, reorganizes the whole system around a simpler truth: when interfaces are standardized and costs are visible, the toll booth loses its power.

That is not a possibility. It is the trajectory.

If I were building a new ad auction protocol, I would publish the mechanism design in public because proprietary market rules create larger liabilities than most founders price in. That claim does not depend on secrecy being always wrong; it depends on auctions governing distribution, pricing, and exclusion rather than merely implementing a feature. Once the mechanism decides which businesses scale, which messages spread, and which actors are quietly priced out, hiding it stops looking like product strategy and starts looking like private rulemaking without accountability.

The usual defense of secrecy is that disclosure lets competitors copy the system. That is true only at the level of rules, because rivals still cannot instantly copy implementation quality, data pipelines, network density, developer ecosystem, operational reliability, or trust. If disclosure destroys your position anyway, the business was resting on obscurity rather than on capabilities that survive inspection.

That weakness gets worse because the secrecy does not hold evenly. If the protocol is effective, sophisticated buyers, large sellers, brokers, researchers, and eventually regulators will reverse-engineer it, which means the parties left uninformed are smaller participants, independent auditors, and the public. Proprietary mechanism design therefore tends to produce asymmetric secrecy rather than real secrecy, and the hidden cost falls on the actors least able to infer the rules from market behavior.

Publishing the mechanism changes the object of trust from company assurances to verifiable incentives. Once participants can reason about the rules, researchers can identify edge cases, outsiders can surface adversarial strategies, and incentive-layer bugs can be examined before they become market structure. That matters because a software bug usually interrupts a service, while a mechanism bug can distort bids, rankings, and growth paths for years without ever presenting as a clean outage. The boundary is that disclosure helps only if the rules are specific enough to audit; vague public narratives still leave the incentive layer effectively private.

That requirement feeds back into internal discipline. If a company knows it must explain the auction in plain language, hidden reserves, favoritism toward owned demand, quietly shifting scoring weights, opaque quality multipliers, and "temporary" exceptions become harder to defend even inside the firm. Public design therefore constrains a common failure mode: solving business pressure by smuggling policy choices into math where outsiders cannot contest them. Many firms avoid openness for exactly that reason, which means the claimed competitive rationale often masks a preference for discretionary control.

The strongest remaining objection is gaming. Publishing the mechanism does increase the chance that bidders and rivals optimize directly against it, but that pressure reveals whether the design is actually robust. An auction that fails once smart participants understand it was depending on ignorance to function, and ignorance is not a stable operating condition for infrastructure. The relevant stress test is therefore not whether the rules survive secrecy, but whether they still work after adversaries study them. If that standard feels too harsh, the market is already too important to be governed by the mechanism.

That standard forces harder work: formalizing which incentive properties hold, stating which ones do not, documenting tradeoffs, and exposing enough detail that criticism can be specific. The tradeoff is real because this slows product discretion and narrows the space for ad hoc fixes. It is still the only path that produces infrastructure worth trusting, because a system that needs confusion in order to remain effective cannot ask for legitimacy once participants become sophisticated.

The case for openness becomes sharper as ad infrastructure converges with AI infrastructure. When a small number of AI companies mediate search, agent actions, shopping, publishing, and ad distribution, governments will not only regulate them but also try to incorporate them through partnership, pressure, or both. That dynamic matters because the state prefers systems it can read and steer, while a secret auction lets steering happen through undisclosed ranking and pricing changes rather than through public rules.

A proprietary mechanism lowers the cost of that capture. If the real governance of the market happens in private meetings between the platform, large customers, and regulators, policy can be inserted as calibration changes, quality adjustments, category exceptions, or suppression rules without public debate. The company can then defend opacity by claiming that disclosure would compromise integrity, which converts a political choice into a technical black box. An open protocol does not prevent capture, but it changes the evidence: once versions and parameters are public, researchers can compare revisions, participants can name the exact change that harmed them, and neutrality claims can be checked against actual rules. That does not remove conflict; it makes conflict specific enough to contest.

The censorship question follows from the same structure. Censorship is easiest when one company owns the mechanism, the client, and the marketplace, because pressure then needs to be applied at only one control point and can be disguised as safety, quality, or platform integrity. In that arrangement, publishers cannot prove discrimination and buyers cannot separate fraud prevention from viewpoint suppression because the ranking logic is both centralized and opaque. Closed systems therefore become censorship machines even when run by decent people, since the failure is structural before it is personal.

Open protocols change the geometry only if the openness extends beyond publication to interoperability. If auction rules are public, implementations can be independent, interfaces are standardized, and participants can move among compatible marketplaces, then blocking becomes harder to hide and harder to universalize because the protocol remains available even when one operator refuses access. One company can still censor and a government can still coerce, but exit becomes possible, and exit is the practical defense that benevolence cannot replace. Email resists exclusion better than social platforms, the web resists enclosure better than app stores, and open financial rails resist cutoff better than private payment stacks because leaving one host does not require leaving the network. Ad protocols inherit that advantage only if compatibility is real enough to let displaced participants go somewhere else.

None of this makes openness a magic spell. Publishing the mechanism will not stop collusion, spam, low-quality inventory, political pressure, or dominance derived from scale, which means bad actors can still win within an open system. What openness changes is the foundation: it makes the market contestable rather than merely administered, and contestability is what turns power into something participants can challenge instead of merely endure.

Companies that reject this usually describe their choice as protection of innovation. In practice, the protected asset is often discretion: the ability to tune rules privately, monetize information asymmetry, and negotiate power case by case. That can be profitable in the short run, but it degrades the market from the inside because each opaque intervention teaches participants that outcomes depend less on rules than on access.

So the actual choice is not between generosity and self-interest. It is between near-term leverage from opacity and long-term credibility built on execution that survives scrutiny. I would choose the second path, and I would publish it as a blog series rather than a forgotten PDF, because the equations alone do not reveal the governing constraints. The design needs to state why this objective function beats another one, why anti-manipulation limits take this form, why relevance, price, and user experience are balanced this way, why governance changes require notice, and why parameter updates are versioned. Without that layer, publication exposes formulas but leaves the politics of the mechanism implicit.

If the protocol is important enough to shape who gets seen, heard, and funded, hiding it treats participants as subjects of a system they are not allowed to inspect. Publishing it treats them as peers who can evaluate the rules before they accept them. Any company that wants the power of infrastructure without that reciprocal burden is asking for deference it has not earned.

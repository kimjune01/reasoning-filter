Transparency does not coexist with opacity in ad exchanges. It kills it.

Incumbents still talk as if transparency were a feature they can meter out: a dashboard here, a heavily caveated report there, maybe enough disclosure to calm buyers without changing the underlying economics. That is wishful thinking. In a market, transparency is not an add-on. It is a state change.

Once buyers and sellers can see what used to be hidden, they do not simply feel better informed. They change behavior. They renegotiate. They reroute spend. They rewrite procurement rules. They stop tolerating vague explanations for margins that only existed because nobody could inspect them. Most of all, they start comparing venues on the terms that matter: price, fill, quality, latency, fraud, take rate. After that, the old story does not come back. Nobody who has seen the spread goes back to enjoying the myth.

That is why exchange fees, wherever transparency becomes real, are headed down, and not a little. Not from 30% to 20%. Down toward something like 2%, with genuinely differentiated services priced separately. The core matching and routing function will get priced like infrastructure, not sorcery.

This only sounds radical because digital advertising has spent years pretending it was exempt from ordinary market logic.

The right analogy is the shipping container. Before containers, moving goods was expensive partly because every transfer point was its own little fiefdom. Different handling, different procedures, different delays, different opportunities for theft, damage, and rent extraction. The container did not just make shipping cheaper. It standardized the unit. That forced every adjacent system to adapt: ports, cranes, ships, rail, warehouses. Once enough of the network retooled around that standard, the economics changed for good. Nobody got to retreat to bespoke cargo handling because the old margins were nicer.

Transparency does something similar in ad markets. It standardizes visibility into what is being bought, what is being sold, who is taking a cut, and what the thing actually does after every intermediary has had its turn. Once that happens, inventory starts to look less like a black box and more like freight in a containerized system: comparable, routable, switchable.

That is when margins collapse.

High exchange fees survive on darkness. They need complexity, asymmetry, and enough switching pain to keep everyone in place. They need buyers to believe the exchange is doing something uniquely difficult and sellers to believe they have nowhere else to go. They need just enough ambiguity that nobody can cleanly separate the commodity function from the premium narrative wrapped around it.

But in a transparent market, matching buyers and sellers is not a 30% business. Not over and over. Not at internet scale. Not once the numbers show how much of the fee pays for real value and how much pays for historical leverage.

The usual defense is that the market is sticky. Fine. It is. That still does not save the fee stack.

Switching costs matter, but they do not have to disappear for margins to compress. They only have to become legible. The moment a customer can say, with decent confidence, “Moving will cost us X, staying will cost us Y every year,” the platform loses its mystique. It becomes a spreadsheet problem.

That is a bad place to be when you charge 30%.

Suppose moving is ugly. Suppose it costs a large advertiser millions in engineering work, retraining, procurement friction, temporary performance risk, all of it real. A recurring 30% tax on serious media volume still turns that pain into an investment case. The customer may not leave tomorrow, but they do not need to. They can split spend, demand rebates, support standards, fund adapters, build internal leverage. Fee compression rarely begins with a clean exit. It begins with the first credible outside option.

That is also why open protocols matter more than incumbents like to admit. Not because openness is morally superior. Because it lowers coordination costs.

A closed platform says: use my tools, my taxonomy, my measurement, my roadmap, and if you want to leave, try carrying all that state with you. An open protocol says: here is the interface; compete on implementation. That is a profound shift. It moves competition away from gatekeeping and toward execution.

Closed systems usually win first. They are easier to launch, easier to orchestrate, better at hiding ugly details in the middle. For a while, they feel like simplification. Then the market grows up, and the simplification curdles into toll collection. Integration difficulty becomes the moat. Proprietary metrics become the moat. Data lock-in becomes the moat. Opaque pricing becomes the moat.

At that point the platform is no longer coordinating the market. It is taxing it.

We have seen this pattern before. Email beat proprietary messaging networks. The web beat online services. TCP/IP beat vendor-specific stacks. Linux outlasted endless attempts to own the internet’s operating system layer. Open does not win instantly. It wins when enough participants realize that interoperability compounds faster than control.

Ad exchanges are especially exposed to that dynamic because what they do is much closer to logistics than to art. Route demand. Expose supply. Clear transactions. Enforce standards. Report outcomes. Those are necessary functions. Necessary functions do not automatically deserve luxury margins.

The container analogy matters here too. Containerization did not erase profit from shipping. It relocated it. Some stayed in transport. Some moved to scale operators, software, financing, warehousing, coordination. But the basic act of moving a standardized unit through an interoperable network got dramatically cheaper.

Transparency will do the same thing in advertising. It will not abolish profit. It will abolish the kind of profit that hides inside complexity and dares nobody to check the math.

Exchanges will still be able to charge for real differentiation: fraud prevention, identity resolution where legal, forecasting, curation, guarantees, analytics, workflow software, credit, settlement, brand safety. Good. Charge for those. But charge for them explicitly, as products. Do not bury them inside a swollen base tax on every transaction.

The base transaction should be cheap.

Why 2%? Not because 2% is sacred. Because once a function is transparent, standardized, and contestable, it gets dragged toward infrastructure pricing. If several providers can clear similar transactions through common protocols, and customers do not have to rebuild their company to switch, then price converges toward cost plus a modest margin. In digital markets, marginal costs are low, software is replicable, scale is high. Those are not the foundations of a permanent 30% rake unless the intermediary is doing something truly irreplaceable.

Usually it is not. Usually it is living off old integration scars and old information asymmetries.

Those decay. First slowly, then very fast.

The incumbent response is easy to predict. They will say transparency confuses customers. They will say the data gets misread. They will say hidden complexity is necessary complexity. They will say outcomes matter more than fee purity. They will say the bundle performs better because it is integrated. Some of that will even be true. It still does not alter the direction of travel. Once the market can compare, pressure on the fee stack stops being episodic and becomes structural.

And transparency does not have to be perfect to do this damage. It only has to be good enough for buyers and sellers to notice that spreads are wider than they were told and alternatives are more plausible than they were supposed to be. That bar is lower than incumbents want to believe.

There is a second-order effect that matters just as much. Opaque ad markets do not merely overpay intermediaries; they distort behavior everywhere else. Publishers optimize for whatever pipe monetizes most easily, not necessarily for what serves audiences. Buyers optimize around platform quirks instead of underlying media quality. Measurement becomes political. Attribution becomes negotiable. Everyone invests too much in gaming the market because nobody fully trusts the market.

Transparency is not a cosmetic reform. It is the moment mythology has to answer to economics.

Economics is not sentimental.

If one exchange can operate profitably at 5%, another will try 4%. If interoperability lowers migration pain, someone will try 3%. If settlement, routing, and reporting become standardized enough, somebody will make money at 2%. A few premium environments may hold higher. Some managed services will absolutely charge more. But the core exchange layer, the basic function of sitting in the middle and clearing trades, will get pushed toward commodity pricing because transparent markets are hard on middlemen.

Not overnight. But not reversibly either.

That distinction matters. “Irreversible” does not mean sudden. Containers did not remake global shipping in a quarter. Open protocols did not erase proprietary networks in a year. But once the better organizing principle appears, the old model stops being the future. It becomes a legacy revenue stream waiting to be competed away.

That is where opaque, high-fee ad exchanges are headed.

The moment transparency becomes credible, the countdown starts. Buyers get more disciplined. Sellers get less captive. New entrants build to the protocol instead of begging for platform permission. Procurement gets involved. Finance gets involved. Engineers get involved. The fee stack, once hidden under jargon and workflow inertia, becomes visible enough to attack.

And once a market starts attacking a 30% take rate, it does not stop at 20% out of courtesy.

It keeps going until the price looks like the function.

For the exchange layer, that number is a lot closer to 2% than 30%. Not because anyone becomes virtuous. Because when interfaces are standardized and costs are visible, the toll booth loses its magic.
